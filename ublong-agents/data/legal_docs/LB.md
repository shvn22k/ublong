# Lebanon — Birth Registration for Refugee and Stateless Children

> Simplified caseworker reference for children born to Syrian refugees, Palestinian refugees, and stateless parents in Lebanon. This is operational guidance for triage, not legal advice.

## Legal and policy basis

- Decree 8837 of 1932 and the Law of Personal Status records govern civil registration in Lebanon, administered by the Personal Status Department of the Ministry of Interior and Municipalities (Foreigners' Registry / Noufous).
- Decree 3260 of 1969 governs the personal status of foreigners in Lebanon and is the basis for registering the births of non-Lebanese, including refugees.
- MoI / General Security facilitation circulars (notably the 2017 and 2018 measures) waived certain fees and relaxed the legal-residency precondition for the late registration of Syrian refugee children, easing the one-year deadline.
- Children born to UNHCR-registered Syrian refugees and to stateless parents are eligible to have the birth recorded even where parents lack Lebanese residency papers.

## Important distinction

Registering a birth in Lebanon proves the parent-child link and the facts of birth; it does not confer Lebanese nationality. Lebanon follows jus sanguinis through the father. A child born to a foreign father is foreign; completing registration is nonetheless critical to prevent statelessness and to preserve the child's link to the parents' nationality.

## Who this pathway applies to

- Children born in Lebanon to Syrian refugee parents registered with UNHCR.
- Children of Palestinian refugees (registration may run through UNRWA records).
- Children of stateless parents at risk of inheriting statelessness.

## Step-by-step registration pathway

1. Obtain the birth notification (notification of birth) from the hospital, clinic, or attending midwife where the delivery took place.
2. Register the birth with the local Mukhtar (neighbourhood registrar), who issues a birth certificate (extract) based on the notification and witness testimony.
3. Have the birth recorded at the Personal Status (Noufous) office for foreigners; for late or complex cases this may require the Mukhtar's certificate plus parents' identity and marriage documents.
4. Where the standard one-year deadline has passed, file a late-registration request relying on the MoI / General Security facilitation measures and submit supporting evidence and an explanation for the delay.
5. For stateless or undetermined-nationality cases, seek legal-aid support and pursue nationality determination separately so the child's status is clarified.

## Required documents

- UNHCR Registration Certificate (or UNRWA record for Palestinian refugees).
- Hospital or clinic birth notification, or a midwife's birth record.
- Mukhtar family-status affidavit / birth certificate extract.
- Parents' identity documents and, where applicable, marriage certificate.

## Accepted substitutes when originals are unavailable

- Hospital birth notification → midwife (daya) record plus two witness statements before the Mukhtar.
- Parents' national ID or passport → UNHCR registration certificate together with any available civil documents.
- Marriage certificate (when the marriage is unregistered) → religious marriage document plus witness testimony; unregistered marriages should be flagged as they complicate paternal filiation.

## Submission office

Local Mukhtar for the certificate extract, then the Personal Status (Noufous / Foreigners' Registry) office of the Ministry of Interior, in the district of birth.

## Estimated timeline

Typically 4–8 weeks; late registration after the one-year deadline takes longer and may require legal-aid representation.

## Special notes and common blockers

- Late registration: births not recorded within one year historically required a court judgment, but facilitation circulars have eased this for Syrian refugees — cite the relevant MoI measure.
- Unregistered or religious-only marriages frequently block recording of the father's details; flag for legal-aid support.
- Undocumented parents: missing residency papers should not, under the facilitation measures, prevent recording the birth — escalate if an office refuses on that basis.
