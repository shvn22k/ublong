# Kenya — Birth Registration for Refugee and Stateless Children

> Simplified caseworker reference for children born to refugee and stateless parents in Kenya, with focus on the Dadaab and Kakuma camps and Kalobeyei settlement. This is operational guidance for triage, not legal advice.

## Legal and policy basis

- Births and Deaths Registration Act (Cap. 149) governs civil birth registration in Kenya, administered by the Civil Registration Services (CRS) under the State Department for Immigration and Citizen Services.
- The Constitution of Kenya 2010, Article 53, guarantees every child the right to a name and nationality from birth and to birth registration.
- Refugees Act 2021 and the Refugees Regulations provide the framework for refugees and asylum seekers, administered by the Department of Refugee Services (DRS, formerly RAS); it supports civil documentation for refugees, including birth registration.
- Births should be registered within 6 months of birth; registration after this period is treated as late registration and requires supporting evidence.

## Important distinction

A Kenyan birth certificate registers the fact of birth and the child's particulars; it is not by itself proof of Kenyan citizenship. For children of refugees it is nonetheless essential for protection, school enrolment, health access, and any future status or resettlement process.

## Who this pathway applies to

- Children born in the Dadaab or Kakuma camps or Kalobeyei settlement to registered refugee parents.
- Children of stateless parents or of undetermined nationality.
- Late-registration cases where the birth was not recorded within six months.

## Step-by-step registration pathway

1. Obtain a birth notification from the health facility (the Notification of Birth, Form B/“acknowledgement of birth”) where the delivery occurred. For home or camp births without a facility record, obtain a statement from the traditional birth attendant.
2. Obtain a refugee attestation letter from UNHCR or the camp administration confirming the parents' registered status.
3. Present the notification and attestation to the Assistant Chief / Chief's office or the sub-county Civil Registration office serving the camp.
4. Complete the birth registration application; the Civil Registrar records the birth and issues the birth certificate.
5. For late registration (after 6 months), submit the late-registration application with secondary evidence (health records, baptismal card, school records, or sworn affidavits) and an explanation for the delay.
6. Where nationality is undetermined, coordinate with the Department of Refugee Services so the child's status is documented.

## Required documents

- UNHCR refugee attestation letter or the parents' refugee identity cards.
- Health facility birth notification (Notification of Birth).
- Parents' identity / registration documents and proof of camp registration.
- Completed birth registration application form.

## Accepted substitutes when originals are unavailable

- Health facility birth record → affidavit from the traditional birth attendant (TBA) plus a supporting statement from the camp administration or block leader.
- Parents' national passport or ID → UNHCR refugee attestation letter and the camp registration card.
- Proof of birth date for older children (late registration) → baptismal card, immunisation/clinic card, or school records as secondary evidence.

## Submission office

Assistant Chief / Chief's office or the sub-county Civil Registration Services office serving Dadaab, Kakuma, or Kalobeyei; coordinate with the Department of Refugee Services where status is undetermined.

## Estimated timeline

Typically 3–6 weeks with complete documentation; late registration with secondary evidence takes longer.

## Special notes and common blockers

- Missing health facility record is common for camp and home births and is routinely resolved with a TBA affidavit and camp-administration support.
- Late registration beyond six months requires secondary evidence and a clear explanation for the delay.
- Undetermined nationality: register the birth first to secure the child's documentation, then pursue status clarification with the Department of Refugee Services.
